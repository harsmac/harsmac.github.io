From VAE's Blur Problem to Wasserstein Distance — why adversarial training works

Harshitha Machiraju · 20 min read
Part I

Why GANs? The VAE Problem

1 VAE's Blurry Outputs: Why MSE Sucks

VAE works, but outputs are blurry. Why?

The Culprit: Pixel-wise Reconstruction Loss

VAE's reconstruction term is MSE:

$$\mathcal{L}_{recon} = \|x - \hat{x}\|^2 = \sum_i (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2$$

This penalizes each pixel independently. The problem:

Ground truth: Sharp whisker at pixel (50, 73) Decoder uncertainty: "Whisker could be at (50, 73) or (50, 74)... not sure" What MSE rewards: Output 0.5 gray at BOTH pixels This minimizes expected squared error! Result: Blurry whisker

When uncertain about exact positions, MSE rewards hedging — outputting the average. Averages are blurry.

2 The GAN Insight: Learn the Metric

Key Insight

We can't write down the perfect IQA metric, but we can train a neural network to approximate human judgment.

VAE

"Minimize pixel difference from ground truth"

Hand-crafted loss (MSE)

→ Blurry outputs

GAN

"Fool a critic that's trying to spot fakes"

Learned loss (Discriminator)

→ Sharp outputs

4 The Minimax Game

$$\min_G \max_D \quad \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$
  • D maximizes (better at classifying)
  • G minimizes (better at fooling)

5 Training: Why Alternate Updates?

# Each training step: # Step 1: Train D real_images = next(dataloader) z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim) fake_images = G(z).detach() # detach: don't update G here loss_D = -torch.mean(torch.log(D(real_images))) \ -torch.mean(torch.log(1 - D(fake_images))) loss_D.backward() optimizer_D.step() # Step 2: Train G z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim) fake_images = G(z) loss_G = -torch.mean(torch.log(D(fake_images))) # fool D loss_G.backward() optimizer_G.step()
🤔 Question

"Why alternate? Why not update both simultaneously?"

✓ Answer

They have opposite goals — D maximizes, G minimizes. Simultaneous updates fight over the same computation graph. Alternating gives each a stable target.

Part III

The Theory

6 What G Actually Minimizes: JS Divergence

Given optimal discriminator $D^*(x) = \frac{p_{data}(x)}{p_{data}(x) + p_g(x)}$, plugging back into the objective gives:

$$V(G, D^*) = -\log 4 + 2 \cdot D_{JS}(p_{data} \| p_g)$$
Key Insight

G is minimizing the Jensen-Shannon divergence between real and generated distributions!

Part IV

GAN Problems

7 Mode Collapse

Real data has multiple modes: Mode A (faces) Mode B (cats) Mode C (cars) ███ ███ ███ Mode collapse: G only produces one mode Generated: All cats ███ ███ ███ ███ ███ ███ ███ ███ (all from Mode B)

G found one thing that fools D and keeps producing it. D keeps catching that thing. Neither improves.

8 The JS Gradient Problem

🤔 Question

"Wait, why is JS divergence a problem?"

JS is bounded between 0 and log(2). When distributions don't overlap:

$$D_{JS}(p_{data} \| p_g) = \log 2 \quad \text{(constant)}$$

Constant = zero gradient. Early in training, G's distribution is far from real data with no overlap → JS is constant → no learning signal!

Part V

Wasserstein to the Rescue

9 Earth Mover's Distance

KL/JS perspective: "How different are the probability values at each point?" Non-overlapping → infinite/constant → useless gradient Wasserstein perspective: "How much work to move probability mass from one distribution to the other?" Non-overlapping → still finite distance → useful gradient!
$$W(p, q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(p, q)} \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \gamma}[\|x - y\|]$$

10 Why Wasserstein Gives Better Gradients

Non-overlapping distributions: p_data: ████████ gap p_g: ████████ JS divergence: log(2) → log(2) → log(2) (constant, zero gradient) Wasserstein: 100 → 50 → 10 (decreases as gap closes, useful gradient!)
Key Insight

Wasserstein distance measures actual geometric distance between distributions. Even when they don't overlap, moving them closer reduces the distance.

11 WGAN

WGAN approximates Wasserstein distance using a "critic" (not discriminator):

$$W(p_{data}, p_g) \approx \max_{\|D\|_L \leq 1} \left[ \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{data}}[D(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[D(G(z))] \right]$$

The constraint $\|D\|_L \leq 1$ means D must be 1-Lipschitz. Enforced via:

MethodHow It WorksProblem
Weight clippingClamp weights to [-c, c]Destroys capacity
Gradient penalty (WGAN-GP)Add $(\|\nabla D\| - 1)^2$ to lossWorks well ✓
Spectral normalizationNormalize by largest singular valueWorks well ✓

13 GAN Latent Space: No Guarantees Like VAE

🤔 Question

"Do GANs guarantee smooth interpolation like VAE?"

✓ Answer

No theoretical guarantee. GAN has no encoder and no constraint forcing coverage of the latent space.

🎯 Interview Narrative

"VAE uses MSE which is pixel-wise and doesn't match human perception — it rewards blurry averages. GANs replace this with a learned loss: the discriminator learns what 'real' looks like from data, then guides the generator.

The minimax game makes G minimize JS divergence between real and generated distributions. But JS has a problem: it's constant when distributions don't overlap, giving zero gradient early in training.

Wasserstein distance fixes this by measuring actual transport cost between distributions. Even non-overlapping distributions get meaningful gradients. WGAN uses a Lipschitz-constrained critic to approximate Wasserstein distance.

Unlike VAE, GAN has no encoder and no guarantee of smooth latent space. The KL term in VAE forces coverage of N(0,I); GAN has no such constraint, making mode collapse and dead zones possible."
